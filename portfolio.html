Turn class projects into evidence of what you can do.

A portfolio helps employers understand how you think, how you solve problems, and how your skills translate into real work. Focus on clarity, process, and reflection.

Open the (UX) Portfolio Guide Portfolio Rubric

What to include in a strong portfolio

  • Three to six focused projects: choose work that shows a range of skills without overwhelming the reader.
  • Your role: clearly explain what you owned, especially for team projects.
  • Your process: show how you researched, designed, analyzed, built, or tested.
  • Your outcome: explain what changed, what you learned, or what decision the work supported.
  • Accessible presentation: use headings, readable text, alt text, and clear navigation.

Case study structure

1. Context

What was the project, who was it for, and what problem were you trying to solve?

2. Approach

Describe your methods, constraints, and the choices you made along the way.

3. Deliverables

Show the artifact, prototype, model, or output and explain why it matters.

4. Reflection

Share what you learned and how the project changed your thinking or skill set.

Portfolio FAQ

  • Do I need a portfolio for every role? Not always, but it is especially useful for UX, design, product, and technical roles.
  • Can I use class projects? Yes. Class work is often your best evidence when it is explained clearly.
  • Should I include unfinished work? Only if you can explain what you learned and why the work still demonstrates a skill.
